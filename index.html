First Prelim

First Prelim - Module 1: Introduction to Human Behavior & Victimology

I. Defining Human Behavior in Criminology

Human Behavior refers to the range of every physical action and observable emotion associated with individuals. In criminology, we move beyond simple observation to analyze the biological, psychological, and social motivations behind each act.

  • Psychology: The scientific study specifically addressing how internal mental states and external environmental stimuli interact to produce behavior.
  • Behavioral Patterns: Actions are classified into Overt (visible physical actions like speech) and Covert (internal mental processes such as cognition, memory, and emotional regulation).
  • Human Development: The longitudinal study of growth. Understanding developmental deviance helps criminologists identify early intervention points for individuals at risk.

II. Goals of Psychology

Psychology aims to understand the "What," "Why," and "How" of human behavior through four fundamental goals:

  • Describing Behavior: Systematically defining the difference between typical and atypical behavior to create accurate assessment tools.
  • Explaining Behavior: Determining the "why" behind reactions and understanding how various stimuli affect long-term mental health.
  • Predicting Behavior: Anticipating how an individual's behavior will manifest in the future based on underlying causes and past patterns.
  • Changing Behavior: Modifying or controlling behavior to achieve beneficial, clinical, and lasting changes in an individual.

III. Expanded Scope of Human Behavior (Academic Depth)

To truly understand behavior within the realm of criminal justice, we must view it as a Multi-Dimensional Phenomenon. It is not just "what" someone does, but the culmination of internal biology and external environment colliding in real-time.

1. The Bio-Psycho-Social Framework

Academia uses this holistic model to explain why behaviors occur. It suggests three main pillars:

  • Biological Pillar: Explores brain chemistry, hormones (like testosterone or cortisol), and neurological structures. For example, a prefrontal cortex injury can lead to impulse control issues.
  • Psychological Pillar: Focuses on personality traits, cognitive processing, and emotional regulation. This includes how one perceives a threat or justifies an action.
  • Social Pillar: Examines peer pressure, family dynamics, and socioeconomic status. A person's "Nurture" (environment) can often trigger or suppress their "Nature" (genetics).

2. Fundamental Characteristics of Human Behavior

Behavioral Traits & Characteristics
Characteristic Description Criminological Importance
Predictability Most behaviors follow established patterns over time. Allows experts to perform "Risk Assessments" for recidivism.
Adaptability Behavior changes based on the rewards or punishments from the environment. Forms the basis of Correctional Rehabilitation and Behavior Modification.
Goal-Oriented Even irrational behavior usually serves a perceived need or desire. Helps investigators identify the "Motive" behind a crime.

3. Real-Life Applications & Examples

How do we apply these theories in the real world? Here are a few critical examples:

  • Criminal Profiling

    By analyzing how a crime was committed (overt behavior), profilers can infer the personality and mental state (covert traits) of the perpetrator.

  • Victimology Research

    Studying the behavior of victims helps identify "Vulnerability Factors." For instance, lifestyle choices can sometimes increase the statistical likelihood of becoming a victim of certain crimes.

  • Crisis Intervention

    Officers use behavioral knowledge to "de-escalate" situations, recognizing when an individual is acting out due to a mental health crisis (Psychosis) rather than criminal intent.

IV. Advanced Comprehensive Study: Human Behavior & Criminogenics

Moving into advanced academia, we must analyze the Criminogenic Factors—the specific drivers within human behavior that lead to law-breaking. This section explores the deeper machinery of the human mind and society's role in its shaping.

A. The Philosophy of Choice: Rationality vs. Determinism

Criminology is split between two major schools of thought regarding behavior:

  • The Classical School (Free Will): Argues that behavior is a result of rational choice. Individuals weigh the pains vs. pleasures of a crime. If the punishment (pain) is certain and swift, the rational person will choose not to commit the act.
  • The Positive School (Determinism): Argues that behavior is determined by internal and external factors (genetics, poverty, childhood trauma) beyond the individual's control. This school shifts the focus from "punishment" to "treatment and rehabilitation."

B. The Mechanism of Abnormal Behavior

When behavior deviates significantly from social norms, it enters the realm of pathopsychology. Experts look at three internal systems:

  1. Cognitive Dissonance: When a person's behavior contradicts their values, they experience stress. Criminals often resolve this by "Neutralizing" their behavior (e.g., "They deserved it" or "It’s not my fault").
  2. Impulse Control: Behavior driven by the ID (Pleasure Principle) without the filter of the Superego is a primary marker for violent crime.
  3. Learned Helplessness: Repeated exposure to negative environmental stimuli (abuse, extreme poverty) can lead to a behavior of passivity or, conversely, a sudden violent "Acting Out" as a defense mechanism.

V. The Science of Victimology: A Modern Expansion

Victimology is the scientific study specifically addressing the Victim rather than the perpetrator. It is a critical half of the Crim 3 subject, focusing on the theories of victimization and the rights of those affected by crime.

1. Primary Theories of Victimization

Academic Frameworks for Victim Studies
Theory Name Core Concept Scenario/Application
Victim Precipitation The victim's own actions (intentional or not) contribute to the crime taking place. Starting a physical fight that leads to being injured by the other party.
Lifestyle Exposure Certain demographic groups have a higher risk due to their nightly activities or associations. A person working late nights in a high-crime area compared to someone in a secure office.
Routine Activities Crime occurs when a motivated offender, a suitable target, and no capable guardian converge. A house left unlocked (target) while owners are away (no guardian) in a neighborhood with a high burglary rate (offender).

2. The "Victim Paradox" & Responsibility

A complex part of this study is understanding Benjamin Mendelsohn’s classification of victims. He was the first to categorize them by degree of responsibility:

  • The Completely Innocent Victim: No contribution to the crime (e.g., a child or unconscious person).
  • The Victim with Minor Guilt: Being in a place they shouldn't be, leading to a crime.
  • The Victim as Guilty as the Offender: Often seen in "Victimless" crimes like illegal gambling.
  • The Victim more Guilty than the Offender: Instances of "Instigation," where the victim provoked the attacker to the point of violence.

VI. Interdisciplinary Connections: Why this Subject Matters

Study in Human Behavior and Victimology does not exist in a vacuum. It is the bridge between several vital academic fields:

Sociological Integration

We look at Social Bond Theory (Hirschi). If an individual has strong attachments to family, commitment to education, and involvement in prosocial activities, their "Behavioral Output" is statistically unlikely to be criminal. This subject explains why society must invest in social structures to prevent crime before it happens.

Legal Significance (Mens Rea)

Lawyers and Judges must understand behavior to determine Intent (Mens Rea). Was the action a result of a "Rational" plan (Premeditated) or an "Involuntary" response (Crime of Passion)? The distinction determines the severity of the sentence and the possibility of an insanity defense.

Significance Summary: This subject is the "Compass" of the Criminal Justice System. Without understanding the human element, law enforcement becomes merely reactive. With it, we gain the power of Prevention, Effective Rehabilitation, and Proactive Justice.

VII. Classifications of Human Behavior (Comparison Table)

Detailed Classification System
Classification Type Sub-Domain Academic Definition & Context

VIII. Determinants of Human Behavior

What drives us? Academia splits these influences into two primary groups:

  • Hereditary & Biological Foundations
    • Genetic Traits: Inherited predispositions observed in family lineages like the Jukes, Edwards, and Kallikak studies.
    • Temperament: The fundamental, biologically-based groundwork evident early in life that dictates emotional reactivity.
  • Environmental Systems (Bronfenbrenner’s Model)
    • Microsystem: The closest environment (Family, Home, School).
    • Mesosystem: The interconnections between the microsystems.
    • Exosystem: External settings that indirectly affect the individual (e.g., Parent's stress).
    • Macrosystem: The overarching cultural values, laws, and customs.
    • Chronosystem: The influence of time and generational patterns.

IX. Theories of Personality & Development

Freud’s Tripartite Structure

The human psyche is a battlefield of three distinct forces:

ID: The selfish, pleasure-seeking part of the mind (Pleasure Principle).
EGO: The rational part that balances urges and reality (Reality Principle).
SUPEREGO: The moral center; internalizes societal rules.

X. Personality Trait Approaches

  • Gordon Allport: Cardinal (dominant), Central (basic blocks), and Secondary (situational) traits.
  • The Big Five (OCEAN): Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism.

XI. Importance in Criminal Justice

Understanding human behavior is a foundational tool for the CJ system:

  • Criminal Profiling: Narrowing suspect lists through behavior analysis.
  • Investigative Approaches: Detecting deceptions during clinical interrogations.
  • Reducing Recidivism: Developing effective rehabilitation programs.
  • Legal Competency: Assessing the mental state of witnesses and suspects.

Second Prelim - Module 2: Cognitive Processes Influencing Behavior

I. Howard Gardner’s Multiple Intelligences

Intelligence is not a single fixed trait but a collection of distinct abilities:

  • Linguistic: Sensitivity to the meaning and order of words.
  • Logical-Mathematical: Ability to handle long chains of reasoning and recognize patterns.
  • Visual-Spatial: Capacity to perceive the visual-spatial world accurately.
  • Bodily-Kinesthetic: Expertise in using one's whole body to express ideas.
  • Musical: Sensitivity to pitch, melody, and rhythm.
  • Interpersonal: Ability to discern and respond appropriately to the moods of others.
  • Intrapersonal: Knowledge of one's own internal aspects of feelings.
  • Naturalist: Recognition and classification of the numerous species of flora and fauna.

II. Intellectual Disability Levels

Classification of Intellectual Impairment
Level IQ Range Clinical Description
Mild 55–69 "Educable"; can manage unskilled jobs and live independently with little supervision.
Moderate 40–54 "Trainable"; can learn simple routine jobs but usually require a protected environment.
Severe 25–39 Require constant care/attention; usually associated with organic pathology.
Profound < 25 Require complete hospitalization and constant personal care.

III. Theories of Emotion

How we experience emotion is interpreted through several academic lenses:

  • James-Lange Theory: Suggests that the physiological response comes before the emotion (e.g., we feel sad because we cry).
  • Cannon-Bard Theory: Argues that physiological responses and emotional experiences occur simultaneously.
  • Two-Factor Theory (Schachter-Singer): Emotion is the result of a cognitive interpretation of a physiological response.

Self-awareness Self-regulation Motivation Empathy Social Skills

IV. Stress, Conflict, and Frustration

Stress (General Adaptation Syndrome): The body's response to challenge through Alarm (Fight-or-Flight), Resistance (Adaptation), and Exhaustion (Depletion).

Types of Conflict

  • Approach-Approach: Choosing between two desirable goals.
  • Avoidance-Avoidance: Choosing between two unattractive options.
  • Approach-Avoidance: A goal that has both positive and negative consequences.

Frustration & Depression

Frustration: The thwarting of one's desires. Common responses include Aggression, Scapegoating (blaming others), and Escape (withdrawal).

Forms of Depression: Includes Major Depressive Disorder (MDD), Dysthymic Disorder (long-term low mood), Bipolar Disorder (manic-depressive shifts), and Situational Depression (event-triggered).

V. Cognitive Processes: Learning & Memory

How an individual processes information affects their interactions with the world:

  • Types of Learning:
    • Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Learning based on stimulus association.
    • Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Learning based on rewards or punishments.
    • Observational Learning: Learning by imitating behavior seen in others.
  • Memory Structure: Sensory Memory (Brief), Short-Term Memory (Temporary), and Long-Term Memory (Permanent, split into Explicit and Implicit).

Midterm - Module 3: Coping, Defense Mechanisms, and Mental Disorders

Identifying Pathology: The 4 D's

To differentiate behavior from the norm, clinical experts use these academic markers:

  1. Distress: Behavior that causes unpleasant feelings for self or others.
  2. Danger: Behavior that is harmful to self or the community.
  3. Deviance: Behavior that is statistically rare or socially unusual.
  4. Dysfunction: Behavior that interferes with daily living and productivity.

Mental Health Perspectives: Neurosis vs. Psychosis

Clinical Comparison Chart
Feature Neurosis Psychosis
Reality Contact Remains in touch with reality. Loss of contact; delusions/hallucinations.
Symptoms Anxiety, mild depression, phobias. Severe cognitive and emotional deficits.
Functioning Generally functional but distressed. Significant impairment in all life areas.

Coping & Defense Mechanisms

Coping Styles: Problem-focused (solving the stressor) and Emotion-focused (managing the suffering).

Freud’s Defense Mechanisms: Unconscious maneuvers used to protect the ego from anxiety:

  • Acting Out: Expressing mental conflict through physical action.
  • Denial: Refusal to accept reality or fact.
  • Regression: Reverting to an earlier childhood stage.
  • Projection: Attributing one's own impulses onto another person.

Semi Final - Module 4: Delusional Disorder: False Belief

Segment 3: Delusional Disorder — False Belief

Delusional disorder, previously called paranoid disorder, is a type of serious mental illness—called a "psychosis"—in which a person cannot tell what is real from what is imagined. The main feature of this disorder is the presence of delusions, which are unshakable beliefs in something untrue.

People with delusional disorder experience non-bizarre delusions, which involve situations that could occur in real life, such as being followed, poisoned, deceived, conspired against, or loved from a distance. These delusions usually involve the misinterpretation of perceptions or experiences.

Types of Delusional Disorder

Classification of Delusional Types
Type Core Belief / Manifestation
Erotomanic Believes another person, often someone important or famous, is in love with them. May attempt contact or engage in stalking behavior.
Grandiose Has an over-inflated sense of worth, power, knowledge, or identity. May believe they have a great talent or have made an important discovery.
Persecutory Believes they (or someone close) are being mistreated, spied on, or that someone is planning to harm them. May make repeated complaints to legal authorities.
Somatic Believes they have a physical defect or medical problem that does not actually exist.
Mixed Exhibits two or more of the delusional types listed above simultaneously.
Jealous Believes that their spouse or sexual partner is unfaithful, without adequate evidence.

Causes of Delusional Disorder

The exact cause is not yet known, but researchers examine three contributing factor domains:

  • Genetic Factors

    Delusional disorder is more common in people who have family members with delusional disorder or schizophrenia, suggesting a hereditary component passed from parents to children.

  • Biological Factors

    Abnormalities in certain brain areas and an imbalance of neurotransmitters—chemicals that help nerve cells send messages—have been linked to the formation of delusional symptoms.

  • Environmental / Psychological Factors

    Stress, alcohol and drug abuse, and social isolation (e.g., immigrants, those with poor sight/hearing) can trigger or increase vulnerability to developing delusional disorder.

Segment 4: Mood Disorders

If you have a mood disorder, your general emotional state or mood is distorted or inconsistent with your circumstances and interferes with your ability to function. You may be extremely sad, empty or irritable (depressed), or you may have periods of depression alternating with being excessively happy (mania).

Types of Mood Disorders

Mood Disorder Classification
Disorder Description
Major Depressive Disorder Prolonged and persistent periods of extreme sadness.
Bipolar Disorder Also called manic depression; includes alternating times of depression and mania.
Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) Depression associated with fewer hours of daylight from late fall to early spring.
Cyclothymic Disorder Emotional ups and downs that are less extreme than bipolar disorder.
Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder Mood changes and irritability during the premenstrual phase that resolve with the onset of menses.
Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia) A long-term (chronic) form of depression.
Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder Chronic, severe irritability in children with frequent temper outbursts inconsistent with developmental age.
Depression Related to Medical Illness Persistent depressed mood directly related to the physical effects of another medical condition.
Substance/Medication-Induced Depression Symptoms during or soon after substance use, withdrawal, or medication exposure.

Focus: Two Phases of Bipolar Disorder

Manic Phase
Feelings of heightened energy, creativity, and euphoria. The person may talk rapidly, sleep very little, and feel all-powerful, invincible, or destined for greatness.
Depressive Episode
The other side of the bipolar coin. Patients often sleep more than usual, are lethargic, and may show irritability during these episodes.

Segment 5: Personality Disorders

A personality disorder is a type of mental disorder in which you have a rigid and unhealthy pattern of thinking, functioning and behaving. A person with a personality disorder has trouble perceiving and relating to situations and people, causing significant problems and limitations in relationships, social activities, work and school.

The Ten Types of Personality Disorder

Comprehensive Personality Disorder Reference
# Disorder Key Characteristics
1 Borderline Personality Disorder Characterized by self-harm, extremely unstable mood, and a tendency to view oneself or others as wholly good or bad. Marked by chaotic behaviors, emotional instability, and fears of abandonment.
2 Antisocial Personality Disorder Thrives on conflict; disregards social conventions. May engage in verbal or physical altercations with strangers. Abusive in relationships with no remorse. Can lead to substance misuse.
3 Histrionic Personality Disorder Extremely emotional, overwrought, or dramatic behavior to call attention. Excessive attention to appearance and constant seeking of approval.
4 Narcissistic Personality Disorder Feelings of great self-importance masking underlying insecurity. Exploits others without remorse. Extremely sensitive to criticism. May complain of unexplained physical aches.
5 Avoidant Personality Disorder Extreme fear of rejection and criticism leading to inability to engage in meaningful relationships. Feels crushed by perceived foolish behavior. Avoids social situations.
6 Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD) Not the same as OCD. Characterized by perfectionism at the expense of flexibility. Rigid "my way or the highway" attitude toward routines and decision-making.
7 Schizoid Personality Disorder Not the same as schizophrenia. Described as "withdrawn" or "solitary." Appears emotionally cold and preoccupied with internal fantasies over reality. Often labeled as "loners."
8 Schizotypal Personality Disorder Eccentric behavior and bizarre beliefs that ostracize them from others. Often paired with extreme social anxiety. May believe they can read others' minds.
9 Paranoid Personality Disorder Extremely distrustful; perceives all interaction as a threat. Guarded or secretive behavior. May turn violent quickly if feeling ridiculed or threatened.
10 Dependent Personality Disorder Unwillingness or inability to make own decisions. Very submissive and timid. Even inconsequential decisions require input and reassurance from multiple people.

Clinical Note: Personality disorders can often be treated with medications and Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). However, no one should self-diagnose any mental illness. Only a psychiatrist, psychologist, or social worker can diagnose a personality disorder and create a treatment plan.
