Module I

Foundational Concepts & Research

I. Defining Human Behavior in Criminology

Human Behavior refers to the range of every physical action and observable emotion associated with individuals. In criminology, we move beyond simple observation to analyze the biological, psychological, and social motivations behind each act.

  • Psychology: The scientific study specifically addressing how internal mental states and external environmental stimuli interact to produce behavior.
  • Behavioral Patterns: Actions are classified into Overt (visible physical actions like speech) and Covert (internal mental processes such as cognition, memory, and emotional regulation).
  • Human Development: The longitudinal study of growth. Understanding developmental deviance helps criminologists identify early intervention points for individuals at risk.

IV. Expanded Scope of Human Behavior (Academic Depth)

To truly understand behavior within the realm of criminal justice, we must view it as a Multi-Dimensional Phenomenon. It is not just "what" someone does, but the culmination of internal biology and external environment colliding in real-time.

1. The Bio-Psycho-Social Framework

Academia uses this holistic model to explain why behaviors occur. It suggests three main pillars:

  • Biological Pillar: Explores brain chemistry, hormones (like testosterone or cortisol), and neurological structures. For example, a prefrontal cortex injury can lead to impulse control issues.
  • Psychological Pillar: Focuses on personality traits, cognitive processing, and emotional regulation. This includes how one perceives a threat or justifies an action.
  • Social Pillar: Examines peer pressure, family dynamics, and socioeconomic status. A person's "Nurture" (environment) can often trigger or suppress their "Nature" (genetics).

2. Fundamental Characteristics of Human Behavior

Behavioral Traits & Characteristics
Characteristic Description Criminological Importance
Predictability Most behaviors follow established patterns over time. Allows experts to perform "Risk Assessments" for recidivism.
Adaptability Behavior changes based on the rewards or punishments from the environment. Forms the basis of Correctional Rehabilitation and Behavior Modification.
Goal-Oriented Even irrational behavior usually serves a perceived need or desire. Helps investigators identify the "Motive" behind a crime.

3. Real-Life Applications & Examples

How do we apply these theories in the real world? Here are a few critical examples:

  • Criminal Profiling

    By analyzing how a crime was committed (overt behavior), profilers can infer the personality and mental state (covert traits) of the perpetrator.

  • Victimology Research

    Studying the behavior of victims helps identify "Vulnerability Factors." For instance, lifestyle choices can sometimes increase the statistical likelihood of becoming a victim of certain crimes.

  • Crisis Intervention

    Officers use behavioral knowledge to "de-escalate" situations, recognizing when an individual is acting out due to a mental health crisis (Psychosis) rather than criminal intent.

V. Advanced Comprehensive Study: Human Behavior & Criminogenics

Moving into advanced academia, we must analyze the Criminogenic Factors—the specific drivers within human behavior that lead to law-breaking. This section explores the deeper machinery of the human mind and society's role in its shaping.

A. The Philosophy of Choice: Rationality vs. Determinism

Criminology is split between two major schools of thought regarding behavior:

  • The Classical School (Free Will): Argues that behavior is a result of rational choice. Individuals weigh the pains vs. pleasures of a crime. If the punishment (pain) is certain and swift, the rational person will choose not to commit the act.
  • The Positive School (Determinism): Argues that behavior is determined by internal and external factors (genetics, poverty, childhood trauma) beyond the individual's control. This school shifts the focus from "punishment" to "treatment and rehabilitation."

B. The Mechanism of Abnormal Behavior

When behavior deviates significantly from social norms, it enters the realm of pathopsychology. Experts look at three internal systems:

  1. Cognitive Dissonance: When a person's behavior contradicts their values, they experience stress. Criminals often resolve this by "Neutralizing" their behavior (e.g., "They deserved it" or "It’s not my fault").
  2. Impulse Control: Behavior driven by the ID (Pleasure Principle) without the filter of the Superego is a primary marker for violent crime.
  3. Learned Helplessness: Repeated exposure to negative environmental stimuli (abuse, extreme poverty) can lead to a behavior of passivity or, conversely, a sudden violent "Acting Out" as a defense mechanism.

VI. The Science of Victimology: A Modern Expansion

Victimology is the scientific study specifically addressing the Victim rather than the perpetrator. It is a critical half of the Crim 3 subject, focusing on the theories of victimization and the rights of those affected by crime.

1. Primary Theories of Victimization

Academic Frameworks for Victim Studies
Theory Name Core Concept Scenario/Application
Victim Precipitation The victim's own actions (intentional or not) contribute to the crime taking place. Starting a physical fight that leads to being injured by the other party.
Lifestyle Exposure Certain demographic groups have a higher risk due to their nightly activities or associations. A person working late nights in a high-crime area compared to someone in a secure office.
Routine Activities Crime occurs when a motivated offender, a suitable target, and no capable guardian converge. A house left unlocked (target) while owners are away (no guardian) in a neighborhood with a high burglary rate (offender).

2. The "Victim Paradox" & Responsibility

A complex part of this study is understanding Benjamin Mendelsohn’s classification of victims. He was the first to categorize them by degree of responsibility:

  • The Completely Innocent Victim: No contribution to the crime (e.g., a child or unconscious person).
  • The Victim with Minor Guilt: Being in a place they shouldn't be, leading to a crime.
  • The Victim as Guilty as the Offender: Often seen in "Victimless" crimes like illegal gambling.
  • The Victim more Guilty than the Offender: Instances of "Instigation," where the victim provoked the attacker to the point of violence.

VII. Interdisciplinary Connections: Why this Subject Matters

Study in Human Behavior and Victimology does not exist in a vacuum. It is the bridge between several vital academic fields:

Sociological Integration

We look at Social Bond Theory (Hirschi). If an individual has strong attachments to family, commitment to education, and involvement in prosocial activities, their "Behavioral Output" is statistically unlikely to be criminal. This subject explains why society must invest in social structures to prevent crime before it happens.

Legal Significance (Mens Rea)

Lawyers and Judges must understand behavior to determine Intent (Mens Rea). Was the action a result of a "Rational" plan (Premeditated) or an "Involuntary" response (Crime of Passion)? The distinction determines the severity of the sentence and the possibility of an insanity defense.

Significance Summary: This subject is the "Compass" of the Criminal Justice System. Without understanding the human element, law enforcement becomes merely reactive. With it, we gain the power of Prevention, Effective Rehabilitation, and Proactive Justice.

III. Determinants of Human Behavior (Nested List)

What drives us? Academia splits these influences into two primary groups:

  • Hereditary & Biological Foundations
    • Genetic Traits: Inherited predispositions observed in family lineages like the Jukes, Edwards, and Kallikak studies.
    • Temperament: The fundamental, biologically-based groundwork evident early in life that dictates emotional reactivity.
  • Environmental Systems (Bronfenbrenner’s Model)
    • Microsystem: The closest environment (Family, Home, School).
    • Mesosystem: The interconnections between the microsystems.
    • Exosystem: External settings that indirectly affect the individual (e.g., Parent's stress).
    • Macrosystem: The overarching cultural values, laws, and customs.
    • Chronosystem: The influence of time and generational patterns.
Module II

Theories of Personality & Development

Sigmund Freud’s Tripartite Structure

Freud proposed that the human psyche is a battlefield of three distinct forces:

ID: The selfish, pleasure-seeking part of the mind. Operates on the Pleasure Principle.
EGO: The rational part that balances internal urges and reality. Operates on the Reality Principle.
SUPEREGO: The moral center or conscience. It internalizes societal rules of right and wrong.

Trait Approaches & The Big Five (OCEAN)

Beyond Freud, academic psychology uses trait theory to define consistent behavioral patterns:

  • Gordon Allport’s Traits:
    • Cardinal Traits: Rare traits that dominate a person's entire life.
    • Central Traits: The basic building blocks of personality (Shyness, Honesty).
    • Secondary Traits: Characteristics only present in certain situations.
  • The Big Five (OCEAN Model):
      Openness Conscientiousness Extraversion Agreeableness Neuroticism

Cognitive Processes: Learning & Memory

How an individual processes information affects their interactions with the world:

  • Types of Learning:
    • Classical Conditioning (Pavlov): Learning based on stimulus association.
    • Operant Conditioning (Skinner): Learning based on rewards or punishments.
    • Observational Learning: Learning by imitating behavior seen in others.
  • Memory Structure: Sensory Memory (Brief), Short-Term Memory (Temporary), and Long-Term Memory (Permanent, split into Explicit and Implicit).

Module III

Abnormal Behavior & Clinical Insights

Identifying Pathology: The 4 D's

To differentiate behavior from the norm, clinical experts use these academic markers:

  1. Distress: Behavior that causes unpleasant feelings for self or others.
  2. Danger: Behavior that is harmful to self or the community.
  3. Deviance: Behavior that is statistically rare or socially unusual.
  4. Dysfunction: Behavior that interferes with daily living and productivity.

Mental Health Perspectives: Neurosis vs. Psychosis

Clinical Comparison Chart

Functioning Generally functional but distressed. Significant impairment in all life areas.

Coping & Defense Mechanisms

Coping Styles: Problem-focused (solving the stressor) and Emotion-focused (managing the suffering).

Freud’s Defense Mechanisms: Unconscious maneuvers used to protect the ego from anxiety:

  • Acting Out: Expressing mental conflict through physical action.
  • Denial: Refusal to accept reality or fact.
  • Regression: Reverting to an earlier childhood stage.
  • Projection: Attributing one's own impulses onto another person.
